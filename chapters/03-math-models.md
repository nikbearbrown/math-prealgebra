**TL;DR.** A word problem is a story that hides an equation. The skill of *modeling* is the translation — extracting the unknowns, the relationships, and the constraint that closes the system. Once the equation is on the page, chapter 2 solves it. The hard part — and the part that fails — is the translation.

## 1. Chapter opening

A paramedic kneels next to a patient on a stretcher in the back of an ambulance, the doors still open, sirens off. The patient has been given an estimated weight of seventy-two kilograms. The dispatch protocol calls for a drug dosed at 0.1 milligrams per kilogram of body weight. The vial in the paramedic's hand is labeled *5 mg in 10 mL*. The driver is waiting for the paramedic's call. *How many milliliters does she draw?*

She does the arithmetic by hand on the back of a glove. Total dose: $0.1 \times 72 = 7.2$ milligrams. Concentration of the vial: $5 / 10 = 0.5$ milligrams per milliliter. Volume to draw: $7.2 / 0.5 = 14.4$ milliliters.

Three numbers. Three operations. Two minutes from the question to the syringe. Every step had a unit attached, and at the end the units lined up — *milliliters*, the answer the question asked for.

That is what this chapter is about. The paramedic did not solve the equation by squinting at the back of her glove. She set up a model: dose equals weight times rate, volume equals dose divided by concentration. The arithmetic was the easy part. The model — knowing which operation to apply to which numbers, in which order — is the work.

Word problems frighten students because the math arrives without instructions. *Solve* $3x + 5 = 17$ — the operation is named, the structure given. *A salesperson earns \$1{,}500 per month plus \$60 per unit. How many units must she sell to make \$3{,}900?* — no operation named. The reader has to extract the structure herself, then write the equation, then solve. This chapter is about that extraction.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter you should be able to:

- **Apply** a five-step problem-solving strategy: read, name, translate, solve, verify.
- **Translate** word problems involving consecutive integers, percents, simple interest, discount and markup into linear equations.
- **Solve** mixture problems — coins, tickets, solutions, investments — using the structure $\text{amount} \times \text{value} = \text{quantity}$.
- **Solve** uniform-motion problems using $d = rt$, including problems with two travelers.
- **Apply** geometric formulas — triangle angle sum, the Pythagorean theorem, perimeter and area of a rectangle — to set up and solve linear equations.
- **Translate** word problems involving "at least," "at most," and "between" into linear inequalities and solve them.

### Prerequisites

Chapter 2. You should be able to solve any linear equation or inequality without slowing down. The work of this chapter is the *setup*; the solving is muscle memory you should already have.

### Why this chapter matters

Almost no real problem arrives as a clean equation. It arrives as a sentence, a paragraph, a meeting full of constraints, a rough sketch on a napkin. The skill of converting one of those into an equation — the right equation, with the right unknowns named — is the most underrated skill in algebra and the one that pays the most over a working life. A scientist solving a mass-balance problem, a programmer estimating server load, a contractor pricing a job — all of them are doing what this chapter teaches.

## 2. Concept 1 — The five-step strategy

A trial lawyer told me once that the trick to a good cross-examination is having the answer written down before you ask the question. You do not improvise. You prepare. You know what the witness should say, you know what they will say, and the questions are the road that connects the two.

Word problems are similar. The trick is not to *solve* the problem — solving is mechanical once the equation is on the page. The trick is to *organize the problem* so the equation falls out at the end, almost without effort. A fixed procedure does this.

### The procedure

1. **Read the problem.** Twice. The first reading is for the story. The second is for the math. Underline the question. Underline the numbers. Note the units.
2. **Identify what is asked, and name it.** Choose a variable for the unknown. Write a sentence: *Let $x$ be the number of dollars she spent on the deposit.* If two unknowns are related, name one of them in terms of the other.
3. **Translate the relationships into an equation.** Re-read the problem and convert each English clause into algebra. *Five more than twice a number is fifty-three* becomes $2n + 5 = 53$.
4. **Solve the equation.** Use the strategy from chapter 2.
5. **Verify the answer in the original problem.** Substituting back into the equation is not enough; you have to check the answer against the *story*. *Does it make sense that her deposit was \$2{,}500 — is the number on the right scale, the right sign, the right units?* If yes, write the answer in a complete sentence with units.

The strategy looks excessive for one-step problems. It is. It is the safety net for the harder ones, where you would otherwise lose track. Use it on every problem until it becomes automatic; then drop it where it is excessive and keep it where it earns its place.

### A worked example — consecutive integers

> **Problem.** The sum of three consecutive integers is $96$. Find the integers.

> *Step 1.* Read. Three consecutive integers. Their sum is $96$. Find them.
>
> *Step 2.* Let $n$ be the smallest integer. The next two are $n + 1$ and $n + 2$.
>
> *Step 3.* Their sum: $n + (n + 1) + (n + 2) = 96$.
>
> *Step 4.* Simplify: $3n + 3 = 96$. Subtract $3$: $3n = 93$. Divide by $3$: $n = 31$.
>
> *Step 5.* Verify. The three integers are $31, 32, 33$. Their sum is $96$. $\checkmark$
>
> **Answer:** *The three consecutive integers are 31, 32, and 33.*

The strategy paid off in step 2. By naming *the smallest* integer as $n$, the other two became $n + 1$ and $n + 2$ automatically — the *consecutive* relationship was built into the names. A student who instead named the three integers $a$, $b$, and $c$ without relating them would be stuck with one equation and three unknowns.

### The trade-off

The strategy is a discipline. It costs time per problem and it forces you to write things down that, after enough practice, you can do in your head. Beginners benefit from the discipline. Advanced students chafe at it. The right answer is to use the discipline until you can hold the equivalent structure in your head without writing it, then write only the steps that are not obvious.

The danger of skipping the strategy too early is that on a long, twisty problem, you will reach for it and find that you have not practiced it. The discipline is insurance.

### Common misconceptions

- *"I just need to find the answer, not the equation."* The equation *is* the work. An answer without an equation is a guess. An equation without a checked answer is incomplete.
- *"The variable should be the answer to the question."* Often, but not always. Sometimes naming a different unknown — say, the smaller of two related quantities — gives a cleaner equation. Choose the variable that makes the relationships shortest.

## 3. Concept 2 — The big templates: percent, mixture, motion

Most word problems in beginning algebra fit one of a small number of templates. Recognizing the template tells you what equation to write before you have read the problem twice. Three templates cover the majority of what you will see.

### Template A — Percent

A **percent** is a ratio with denominator one hundred. *Per cent* is Latin for *per hundred*. Twenty percent of a quantity is twenty parts of every hundred parts of the quantity, which is the same as multiplying the quantity by $0.20$.

The basic percent equation is:

$$\text{amount} = \text{rate} \times \text{base}.$$

Or, in the language students often see:

$$P = R \times B.$$

The *base* is the whole; the *rate* is the percent expressed as a decimal; the *amount* is the part. *Twenty percent of fifty is ten*: $R = 0.20$, $B = 50$, $P = 10$.

Three flavors of percent problem arise from this one equation, depending on which of the three quantities is the unknown:

- *What is twenty percent of fifty?* Solve $P = 0.20 \times 50$. Answer: $P = 10$.
- *Ten is what percent of fifty?* Solve $10 = R \times 50$. Answer: $R = 0.20 = 20\%$.
- *Ten is twenty percent of what number?* Solve $10 = 0.20 \times B$. Answer: $B = 50$.

**Percent increase and decrease.** When a price goes from \$80 to \$92, the percent increase is *the change as a fraction of the original*:

$$\text{percent change} = \frac{\text{new} - \text{old}}{\text{old}} \times 100\%.$$

Here: $(92 - 80) / 80 = 0.15 = 15\%$. The price increased fifteen percent.

**Discount and markup.** A discount is a percent of the original price subtracted from the price. A markup is a percent of the cost added to the cost. Same equation, different sign on the change.

**Simple interest.** Money invested at a *simple interest rate* $r$ for $t$ years earns interest equal to:

$$I = Prt,$$

where $P$ is the principal (the original amount). After $t$ years the balance is $P + I$. *Simple* interest does not compound — it is computed on the original principal each year, not on the current balance. (Compound interest is treated in chapter 9 of college algebra; for now, take *simple* at its name.)

### Template B — Mixture

A **mixture** problem combines two quantities of different *value densities* into one quantity. The structure is always:

$$\text{amount} \times \text{value} = \text{quantity}.$$

Apply this to each of the two ingredients separately, then to the mixture as a whole. The three resulting expressions usually fit into one equation.

> **Problem.** A pharmacist has a 50% saline solution and pure (sterile) water. She wants 200 mL of 30% saline. How much of each does she combine?

> *Setup.* Let $x$ = mL of 50% saline used. Then $200 - x$ = mL of water. The water contributes 0% saline.
>
> | Ingredient | Amount (mL) | Value (% saline as decimal) | Quantity (mL of saline) |
> | --- | --- | --- | --- |
> | 50% saline | $x$ | $0.50$ | $0.50x$ |
> | Water | $200 - x$ | $0.00$ | $0$ |
> | Mixture | $200$ | $0.30$ | $0.30 \times 200 = 60$ |
>
> *Equation.* The saline in the ingredients equals the saline in the mixture:
>
> $$0.50x + 0 = 60.$$
>
> *Solve.* $x = 120$ mL of saline; $80$ mL of water.
>
> *Check.* $120 \times 0.5 + 80 \times 0 = 60$ mL of saline in 200 mL of mixture, which is $60/200 = 30\%$. $\checkmark$
>
> **Answer:** *The pharmacist combines 120 mL of 50% saline with 80 mL of water.*

The same template applies to coins (amount × value-per-coin = total value), tickets at different prices, mixed-investment portfolios at different interest rates, and grain blends at different costs per pound. The *names* change; the *equation* does not.

### Template C — Uniform motion

For an object moving at constant rate, the relationship is:

$$d = rt$$

where $d$ is distance, $r$ is rate (speed), $t$ is time. *Uniform* means *constant rate*; the equation does not apply when speed varies.

Three standard kinds of motion problem:

- *One traveler, find one missing quantity.* Distance, rate, or time given, the other to be found.
- *Two travelers in opposite directions.* Total distance is the sum of the two individual distances.
- *Two travelers in the same direction (one chasing the other).* The chaser closes the gap at a rate equal to the difference of their rates.

> **Problem.** Two cars leave the same town at the same time, headed in opposite directions on a straight highway. One travels at 55 mph and the other at 65 mph. After how long are they 360 miles apart?

> *Setup.* Let $t$ = hours of travel. Each car has distance $d_i = r_i t$.
>
> | Car | Rate (mph) | Time (h) | Distance (mi) |
> | --- | --- | --- | --- |
> | Slow | $55$ | $t$ | $55t$ |
> | Fast | $65$ | $t$ | $65t$ |
> | Apart | — | — | $55t + 65t = 360$ |
>
> *Equation.* $120t = 360$.
>
> *Solve.* $t = 3$ hours.
>
> *Check.* In three hours: $55 \times 3 = 165$, $65 \times 3 = 195$, total $360$. $\checkmark$
>
> **Answer:** *The cars are 360 miles apart after 3 hours.*

### The trade-off

Templates make problems fast: recognize the shape, write the equation, solve. The trade-off is that templates can become a substitute for understanding. A student who memorizes that *amount × value = quantity* without internalizing why often misapplies the template — multiplies what should be added, treats a value-density as a total. The template is an aid to recognition; the *understanding* is what tells you when the template applies and when the problem is something else.

The deeper move, the one that lets you solve problems no textbook foresaw: *every quantitative situation can be modeled by writing an expression for each kind of thing involved, then writing one equation that says the kinds balance*. Mixture, motion, percent, money, work-rate problems — all variants of the same balance principle.

### Common misconceptions

- *"$25\%$ off then $25\%$ off again is $50\%$ off."* It is not. After the first discount, the price is $75\%$ of the original. After the second, it is $75\%$ of $75\%$ = $0.5625$ of the original — a $43.75\%$ total discount.
- *"If I averaged 30 mph there and 60 mph back, my average speed is 45 mph."* It is not. Average speed is total distance over total time, not the average of two speeds. For a 60-mile round trip, you spent $60/30 = 2$ hours going and $60/60 = 1$ hour returning, total $3$ hours for $120$ miles, average $40$ mph. The harmonic mean, not the arithmetic mean, governs round-trip speeds.
- *"In a mixture, I should add the percentages."* No — you add the *amounts* of the active ingredient (the *quantities* in the right column of the table), not the percentages. Adding percentages directly conflates ratios with quantities and is the most common mixture-problem error.

## 4. Concept 3 — Geometry and inequality applications

Some word problems are not about money or motion. They are about *shapes* — perimeter, area, angle, the relationships among the sides of a triangle. The strategy and templates from Concepts 1 and 2 still apply; only the formulas change. And some word problems involve a *constraint* rather than an equality — *at least*, *at most*, *no more than*. Those become linear inequalities, solved exactly as in chapter 2.

### Three formulas worth holding in working memory

**Triangle angle sum.** The three interior angles of any triangle, in any plane, sum to $180°$.

$$A + B + C = 180°.$$

This single fact lets you find any missing angle if you know the other two — and it lets you set up equations when one angle is described in terms of another.

**The Pythagorean theorem.** In a right triangle (a triangle with one $90°$ angle), the side opposite the right angle (the *hypotenuse*) and the two other sides (the *legs*) satisfy:

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2,$$

where $c$ is the hypotenuse and $a$, $b$ are the legs.

This is a relationship discovered (or codified) by the Pythagoreans in the sixth century BCE, but evidence suggests Babylonian mathematicians had numerical instances of it a thousand years earlier. It is the foundation of distance measurement in two and three dimensions; you will meet it again in chapter 4 (distance in the coordinate plane) and chapter 9 (radical equations).

**Rectangle perimeter and area.**

$$P = 2L + 2W, \qquad A = L \times W.$$

A worked example using two of these:

> **Problem.** The length of a rectangle is 4 feet less than three times its width. The perimeter is 64 feet. Find the dimensions.

> *Setup.* Let $W$ = width in feet. Then $L = 3W - 4$.
>
> *Equation.* $P = 2L + 2W = 64$, so $2(3W - 4) + 2W = 64$.
>
> *Solve.* Distribute: $6W - 8 + 2W = 64$. Combine: $8W - 8 = 64$. Add $8$: $8W = 72$. Divide: $W = 9$.
>
> *Then* $L = 3(9) - 4 = 23$.
>
> *Check.* Perimeter: $2(23) + 2(9) = 46 + 18 = 64$. $\checkmark$ Length is 4 less than three times the width: $3(9) - 4 = 23$. $\checkmark$
>
> **Answer:** *The rectangle is 9 feet wide and 23 feet long.*

### Inequality applications — when "at least" and "at most" enter

Some problems do not specify an exact value but a *constraint*. *Earn at least \$3,900* (the chapter 2 example). *Spend no more than \$50*. *The triangle's perimeter must be less than 30 feet*. These translate to linear inequalities, and the work of solving them is identical to the work of solving linear equations — with the flip rule from chapter 2 §4 active when you multiply or divide by a negative.

> **Problem.** Erin will earn \$1{,}200 per month plus \$45 for each piece she sells. She wants to earn at least \$3{,}000 per month. What is the minimum number of pieces she must sell?

> *Setup.* Let $p$ = pieces sold. Earnings: $1200 + 45p$. Constraint: $1200 + 45p \ge 3000$.
>
> *Solve.* $45p \ge 1800$ ⇒ $p \ge 40$.
>
> *Answer:* *Erin must sell at least 40 pieces.*

The translation table for inequality language:

| English | Inequality |
|---|---|
| at least | $\ge$ |
| no less than | $\ge$ |
| at most | $\le$ |
| no more than | $\le$ |
| more than | $>$ |
| less than | $<$ |
| between $a$ and $b$ | $a < x < b$ (often) |

### The trade-off

Geometric formulas are *named relationships* you must memorize. The triangle angle sum, the Pythagorean theorem, perimeter and area formulas — these are facts about shapes that no derivation from chapter 1 or chapter 2 will produce. The trade-off is that once memorized, they are *exact* and *universal*: every right triangle in every Euclidean plane satisfies $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$, no exceptions.

The reflexive note: these formulas are facts about *Euclidean* geometry. On the surface of a sphere, the angles of a triangle sum to *more* than $180°$. On a saddle-shaped surface (a hyperbolic plane), to less. The Pythagorean theorem fails on both. For terrestrial-scale problems — rectangles, room layouts, ladders against walls — Euclidean is essentially exact. For map-making across a continent, it is not, and surveyors use spherical geometry instead. The formulas of this chapter are the planar special case of a more general story.

### Common misconceptions

- *"$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ works for any triangle."* It does not. Only for right triangles. For other triangles, the *law of cosines* (a topic of trigonometry) generalizes it.
- *"The hypotenuse is the longest side, so $c$ is the largest."* True for right triangles, and a useful sanity check: if you computed $c < a$ or $c < b$, you have made an error.
- *"At most" means $<$.* No — *at most* means $\le$. *Less than* means $<$. The boundary inclusion matters for both writing and grading.

## 5. Integration / Synthesis

A pair of friends drive to a music festival 240 miles away. The driver, having a faster car, averages $r + 10$ miles per hour. The passenger drives the return trip at $r$ miles per hour. The total round-trip driving time is $8$ hours. *What is the slower driver's speed?*

This problem combines all three concepts.

### Concept 1 — strategy and naming

Read: round trip 240 miles each way, two different speeds, total time 8 hours, find slower speed.

Name: let $r$ = the slower driver's speed in mph. Then the faster speed is $r + 10$ mph.

### Concept 2 — uniform motion template

Each leg of the trip is governed by $d = rt$, so $t = d/r$.

| Leg | Distance (mi) | Rate (mph) | Time (h) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Out | $240$ | $r + 10$ | $\dfrac{240}{r + 10}$ |
| Back | $240$ | $r$ | $\dfrac{240}{r}$ |
| Total | $480$ | — | $\dfrac{240}{r + 10} + \dfrac{240}{r} = 8$ |

### Solving — the equation has fractions, so clear them (chapter 2)

Multiply both sides by $r(r + 10)$, the common denominator:

$$240r + 240(r + 10) = 8r(r + 10).$$

Distribute and simplify:

$$240r + 240r + 2400 = 8r^2 + 80r$$
$$480r + 2400 = 8r^2 + 80r$$
$$0 = 8r^2 + 80r - 480r - 2400$$
$$0 = 8r^2 - 400r - 2400.$$

Divide both sides by $8$:

$$0 = r^2 - 50r - 300.$$

This is a *quadratic* equation. Solving it requires the methods of chapter 7 (factoring) or chapter 10 (quadratic formula), so I will state the solution and defer the work: $r = 25 + 5\sqrt{37} \approx 55.4$ mph (the positive root; the negative root is rejected because speed cannot be negative).

### Verification

Out: $240 / 65.4 \approx 3.67$ hours. Back: $240 / 55.4 \approx 4.33$ hours. Total: $\approx 8.00$ hours. $\checkmark$

### What this shows

Three things.

*First*, the strategy and templates of this chapter generate equations that often outrun chapter 2's solving techniques. A motion problem with two travelers and a time constraint produces a *quadratic* equation. That is a feature, not a bug — it is the reason chapter 7 and chapter 10 exist.

*Second*, the *modeling* — the table, the equations for each leg, the constraint — is the part of the work this chapter teaches. The solving technique is just the next chapter.

*Third*, the work of mathematical modeling never really stops. Chapter 4 will model relationships graphically; chapter 5 with systems of equations; chapter 8 with rational expressions; chapter 10 with quadratics. The *what we are modeling* gets more sophisticated. The structure — *write the equations, solve, verify* — does not change.

### Warm-up

1. **(LO 2, easy)** Find three consecutive integers whose sum is $42$.

2. **(LO 2, easy)** What is $35\%$ of $80$?

3. **(LO 3, easy)** A jar holds $30$ coins, all nickels and dimes, totaling \$2.30. How many of each are there?

### Application

4. **(LO 2, medium)** A coat originally priced at \$160 is on sale for \$120. Find the percent discount.

5. **(LO 2, medium)** \$2{,}500 is invested at $4\%$ simple interest. How much interest is earned in $3$ years? What is the total balance after $3$ years?

6. **(LO 4, medium)** Two trains leave the same station at the same time, traveling in opposite directions. The faster train averages $80$ mph; the slower averages $65$ mph. After how long are they $580$ miles apart?

7. **(LO 5, medium)** Two angles of a triangle are equal, and the third is $20°$ greater than each of the equal angles. Find all three angles.

8. **(LO 5, medium)** A right triangle has legs of length $9$ and $12$. Find the length of the hypotenuse.

### Synthesis

9. **(LO 3, harder)** A coffee blend is to be made from beans costing \$8 per pound and beans costing \$14 per pound. The blender wants $50$ pounds of mixture costing \$11 per pound. How many pounds of each kind are used?

10. **(LO 4 + 5, harder)** A boat travels $36$ miles downstream in $2$ hours and the same distance upstream in $3$ hours. Find the speed of the boat in still water and the speed of the current. (Hint: downstream speed = boat speed + current speed; upstream = boat speed − current. This produces *two* equations and *two* unknowns; you can solve by substitution. We will treat systems formally in chapter 5.)

11. **(LO 5 + 6, harder)** A rectangular garden is to have a perimeter of at most $100$ feet, and the length must be exactly $5$ feet more than the width. What is the largest possible width? Set this up as an inequality, solve, and state the answer in a complete sentence with units.

### Challenge

12. **(LO 3, hard)** A solution is $40\%$ alcohol. How much pure alcohol must be added to $50$ mL of the solution to make it $60\%$ alcohol? *Hint: pure alcohol is $100\%$ alcohol; the mixture's total volume changes.*

13. **(LO 4, hard, points to chapter 8)** Mary can paint a room in $4$ hours; her sister can paint the same room in $6$ hours. How long will it take them to paint the room together? *Hint: rates of work add, even though times do not. If you find the equation involves $\frac{1}{x}$, that is a rational equation — the topic of chapter 8.*

## 7. Chapter summary

You came in able to solve linear equations and inequalities. You leave able to *generate* them from word problems.

You know the five-step strategy: read, name, translate, solve, verify. You know that the *naming* step is where most success or failure happens — choosing the right unknown, choosing one that makes the relationships shortest. You know that the *verify* step is not optional; substituting back into the equation catches arithmetic errors, but only checking against the original story catches setup errors.

You know three big templates. Percent: $P = R \times B$, with variants for percent change, simple interest, discount, and markup. Mixture: $\text{amount} \times \text{value} = \text{quantity}$, applied to two ingredients and a target. Motion: $d = rt$, applied to two travelers in opposite directions, the same direction, or a round trip.

You know a small set of geometric formulas — triangle angle sum, Pythagorean theorem, rectangle perimeter and area — and you know they are facts of *Euclidean* geometry, exact for terrestrial-scale problems but generalized for non-Euclidean settings.

You know how to translate constraint language — *at least*, *at most*, *no more than* — into linear inequalities, and how to solve those using the flip rule when needed.

The single most important idea: *every word problem is a balance of quantities*. Identify the quantities, write expressions for each, and write the one constraint that closes the system. The arithmetic is the easy part. The setup is the work.

The most common mistake to watch for: misreading the constraint. *Twice as many oranges as apples* means $O = 2A$, not $A = 2O$. *Five less than three times a number* means $3n - 5$, not $5 - 3n$. Read each clause aloud; identify the larger or starting quantity; write that on the left.

What you should be able to teach someone else: why averaging two speeds gives the wrong average for a round trip, why adding two consecutive percent discounts does not add to the total discount, and why the Pythagorean theorem requires a right triangle.

## 8. Connections forward

Chapter 4 introduces a different kind of model: the graph of a linear equation in two variables. The single-variable equations of this chapter have one solution; two-variable equations have infinitely many solutions, and the graph — a line in the plane — is the picture of all of them. Slope and intercept of that line will let you read the equation's behavior at a glance, faster than algebra alone.

Chapter 5 then asks: what happens when *two* two-variable equations must hold simultaneously? The answer — find the intersection of two lines — is a *system of equations*, and several problems in this chapter (the boat with current, the painting sisters) are systems in disguise. The methods of chapter 5 will solve them cleanly.

Bring the strategy with you. Read, name, translate, solve, verify. Whether the equation is linear, quadratic, or systemic, the strategy never changes.

**What would change my mind:** If a careful study of student performance showed that students who skipped the five-step strategy and jumped directly to setting up equations did better than those who used the strategy, I'd reconsider whether the strategy is pedagogically essential or merely scaffolding-as-ritual. Current evidence suggests the strategy helps; if that evidence shifted, my position would too.

**Still puzzling:** Why do students who can flawlessly solve $5(x - 2) = 3x + 4$ freeze when the same equation arrives wrapped in a paragraph? The mechanical skill exists; the *recognition* skill — pulling the structure out of prose — is somehow separate. Whether this is a generic feature of how mathematical fluency develops, or a specific failure of how word problems are taught, I am not sure.
