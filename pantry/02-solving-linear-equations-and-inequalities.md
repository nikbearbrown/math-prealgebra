# Pantry — Chapter 02, Solving Linear Equations and Inequalities

Reusable ingredients harvested from the chapter draft. Source: OpenStax *Elementary Algebra 2e* Ch. 2 (CC-BY 4.0).

## Scenes

- **Two friends at a coffee shop with a napkin** — used in §1 cold open — the moment of writing $5c + 14 = 42$ on a napkin and solving captures the *act* of algebraic thinking. Reusable for any "writing the unknown down" introduction.
- **Pan balance with weights** — used in §2 cold open — physical balance grounds the four properties of equality. Reusable as a metaphor for any conservation principle.
- **Foot race measured from start vs. finish line** — used in §4 cold open (flip rule) — same race, two reference frames, opposite inequalities. Reusable wherever a sign or direction reverses under reflection.

## Analogies

- **Equation as pan balance** — explains: why the four properties of equality are legal — limits: breaks down for inequalities and the flip rule, and for any equation involving variables that could be zero.
- **Solving as undoing in reverse build order (taking off a sweater)** — explains: why we subtract before dividing in $3x + 5 = 17$ — limits: works only for linear equations; quadratics and beyond require different unwind logic.
- **Flip rule as reflection across zero** — explains: why multiplying both sides of an inequality by a negative reverses the direction — limits: clean geometrically but still feels procedural until the student has manipulated the number line in chapter 1.

## Etymologies

- **Equation** — Latin *aequatio* (a making equal) — the verb hidden in the noun: an equation is the *act* of asserting equality.
- **Conditional / identity / contradiction** — terms imported from logic; *conditional* means *true under some condition*, *identity* means *true under all conditions*, *contradiction* means *true under no condition*.
- **Inequality** — Latin *inaequalitas* (unevenness) — the natural opposite of equation.
- **Flip rule** — informal, no etymology; named for the visible reversal of the inequality symbol.

## Trade-offs named

- **Balance metaphor: concrete grounding vs. limits at the edges.** Beautiful for the four properties of equality; misleading for the flip rule and for variable-coefficient division.
- **General strategy: universal procedure vs. rote application.** Solves every linear equation in finite steps; risks turning students into calculators when used without paired pattern recognition.
- **Inequality solution sets: regions vs. points.** Inequalities describe regions of the number line, but students arrive expecting equation-style single-number answers.

## Scale shifts used

- **Coffee-shop napkin ↔ professional algorithm in software** — purpose: shows that the same balance principle scales from a six-line scribble to industrial computation. Anchored in §1.

## Worked examples

- **$3x + 5 = 17 \Rightarrow x = 4$** — concept: addition + division properties of equality — reusable as: every later "first solve" example.
- **$5(x - 2) = 3x + 4 \Rightarrow x = 7$** — concept: general strategy with parentheses and variables on both sides — reusable as: ch 3 (math models setup), ch 5 (one-equation step in elimination).
- **$\frac{2}{3}x + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{5}{6} \Rightarrow x = 7/8$** — concept: clearing fractions by LCD — reusable as: ch 8 (rational equations).
- **$0.05x + 0.20(100 - x) = 0.10(100)$** — concept: clearing decimals; mixture problem template — reusable as: ch 3 (mixture/concentration applications).
- **Salesperson income inequality** — concept: linear inequality in an applied setting — reusable as: ch 5 (systems of inequalities, deferred), ch 6/7 (revenue and profit setups).

## Reusable structural moves

- **"The strategy is the safety net when pattern recognition fails"** — frame for procedural-vs-insight tension; reusable any time a procedure is taught alongside a heuristic.
- **"Three kinds of linear equation"** (conditional / identity / contradiction) — taxonomy reusable as a model for taxonomy moves in later chapters (e.g., three kinds of system in ch 5).
- **"Mark the flip explicitly"** — pedagogical move; reusable wherever a single procedural moment causes most errors (the negative discriminant in ch 10, the domain-loss in ch 8).
