## Scenes

- **Vermont winter morning, two thermometers** — used in §1 cold open — anchors the idea that the number line is the same line whether labeled in degrees, dollars, or distance. Reusable in any chapter introducing real-valued quantities.
- **Carpenter at the steel tape, lake thermometer, bank balance** — used in §2 cold open (number line) — three concrete instantiations of the same number-line structure. Reusable as a framing device whenever the lesson is "this abstraction sits behind familiar tools."
- **Recipe card with $3 + 4 \times 2$ cups of flour** — used in §3 cold open (PEMDAS) — gives a high-stakes consequence to ambiguous notation. Reusable wherever you need to motivate the cost of imprecise notation (units, statistical formulas, programming operator precedence).
- **$19 \times 6$ as $20 \times 6 - 6$ in your head** — used in §4 cold open (distributive property) — the property arrives as a mental shortcut everyone has already used unnamed. Reusable for any "you've been doing this without naming it" move.
- **Contractor cutting a 16-foot board with kerf waste** — used in §5 synthesis — combines all three concepts into one problem. Reusable as a setup for chapter 2 (solving the same equation explicitly) and chapter 3 (math models).

## Analogies

- **Number line as the spine that runs through the thermometer, tape measure, and bank account** — explains: that integers, rationals, and reals are one continuous structure with different labels — limits: the analogy treats the line as horizontal-and-finite, which mismatches infinite extent and breaks for higher-dimensional numbers (complex, vector).
- **Subtraction as "addition of the opposite"** — explains: why $5 - (-3) = 5 + 3$ — limits: works perfectly for arithmetic, but masks the conceptual distinction between subtraction-as-removal and addition-as-direction. For very young students this can confuse.
- **Three apples plus five apples is eight apples; three apples plus five oranges does not simplify** — explains: like terms — limits: gives an intuitive sort but breaks down when terms involve negative coefficients or zero (zero apples is still apples? zero of any like term is just zero).
- **PEMDAS as a convention agreed in San Francisco and São Paulo** — explains: why a convention is needed and what it buys (universal communication) — limits: in fact different programming languages use slightly different precedence tables, so the analogy breaks at the edges.
- **Distributive property as the engine — read left to right, expansion; right to left, factoring** — explains: why factoring (chapter 7) and expansion (chapter 6) are the same property in two directions — limits: only really illuminates if the student has already met both expansion and factoring; for a first encounter, "engine" is metaphorical.

## Etymologies

- **Integer** — Latin *integer*, "untouched, whole" — illuminates: an integer has no fractional part; it is a *whole* number, not in the sense of nonnegative but in the sense of intact.
- **Negative** — Latin *negare*, "to deny" — illuminates: a negative number denies the positive direction; it is the contradiction of the positive on the same line.
- **Absolute** — Latin *absolutus*, "freed, unconditional" — illuminates: absolute value is the magnitude *freed from* the sign.
- **Variable** vs. **constant** — Latin *variare* (to change) vs. *constare* (to stand firm) — illuminates: a variable is the changing piece; a constant is the unchanging piece. Used directly in the Greg/Alex example.
- **Commutative** — Latin *commutare*, to exchange or move back and forth — illuminates: the property says you can swap the operands.
- **Distributive** — Latin *distribuere*, to allot to several — illuminates: the multiplication is *distributed across* each term in the sum.

## Trade-offs named

- **Number line: visual intuition vs. procedural power.** The line gives you fast intuition for integer addition/subtraction at the cost of breaking down past simple operations (multiplication of fractions, irrationals).
- **PEMDAS: universal communication vs. memorization burden.** The convention buys agreement across every algebra book in every language; it costs an arbitrary tier system that students must learn by heart and that has subtle traps (M and D paired, A and S paired).
- **Properties of real numbers: legal moves vs. obscured insight.** Naming the property licenses every step but hides the *thinking*. "By the distributive property" can be a substitute for understanding rather than evidence of it.
- **Variables: generality vs. concreteness.** A single expression $g + 3$ captures every age Greg can be, but the leverage costs the immediacy of pointing at one specific case.

## Scale shifts used

- **Single steps on the line ↔ light-years across the observable universe ↔ the diameter of a hydrogen atom** — purpose: shows that the same number line holds all of human measurement at once. Anchored in §2.
- **Recipe card ambiguity ↔ programming language precedence tables (Python `**` vs. `^`)** — purpose: shows that the convention this chapter teaches scales up to working software. Anchored in §3.
- **Mental shortcut $20 \times 6 - 6$ ↔ formal expansion $a(b - c) = ab - ac$** — purpose: shows that everyday cleverness is the same operation as formal algebra. Anchored in §4.
