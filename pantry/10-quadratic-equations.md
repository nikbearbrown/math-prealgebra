## Analogies

- **Three solving methods, three lenses on the same object** — explains: factoring shows structure, completing the square shows geometry, formula shows reliability — limits: stretches when one method dominates in practice (formula does most of the work after schooling).
- **Discriminant as predictor** — explains: why one calculation tells you what kind of answer to expect — limits: the threshold cases ($\Delta = 0$ and small $\Delta$) often surprise students.
- **Vertex as extremum** — explains: why optimization problems reduce to finding the vertex — limits: only works for parabolas; calculus is needed for general curves.

## Etymologies

- **Quadratic** — Latin *quadratus* (squared) — illuminates: a quadratic *involves the square of the variable*.
- **Discriminant** — Latin *discriminare* (to distinguish) — illuminates: it *distinguishes* among the cases of solutions.
- **Parabola** — Greek *parabolē* (placement-beside, comparison) — origin in the Greek classification of conic sections.
- **Vertex** — Latin *vertex* (top, summit) — illuminates: the vertex is the *peak* (or trough) of the parabola.
- **Axis of symmetry** — Latin *axis* (axle) + Greek *symmetria* (commensurability, balanced measure).

## Trade-offs named

- **Three solving methods.** Factoring fastest when it works; Square Root Property fastest with no linear term; quadratic formula universal but more arithmetic.
- **Closed-form vs. numerical.** Quadratic formula works for degree 2; closed-form solutions don't exist for degree 5 and up (Abel-Ruffini).
- **Real vs. complex solutions.** Real-only restriction loses information; complex solutions recover the lost roots (deferred to college algebra).

## Scale shifts used

- **Single quadratic ↔ general polynomial theory** — purpose: places this chapter as the simplest non-trivial polynomial case. Anchored in §3 reflexive note about Abel-Ruffini.
