## Scenes

- **Paramedic computing IV dose on the back of a glove** — used in §1 cold open — the moment when modeling is *time-critical* and units must line up. Reusable for any "modeling under pressure" framing.
- **Trial lawyer with the answer written before the question** — used in §2 cold open — frames the strategy as preparation, not improvisation. Reusable for any "discipline-as-insurance" move.

## Analogies

- **Cross-examination as model for word-problem strategy** — explains: why you organize the problem before you start solving — limits: stretches when applied to short one-step problems where setup overhead exceeds the math.
- **Mixture template as a balance of "amount × value"** — explains: why coins, ticket sales, solutions, and investments share one equation shape — limits: collapses when the value depends on amount (price discounts, work-rate problems).

## Etymologies

- **Percent** — Latin *per centum*, "by the hundred" — illuminates: a percent is a ratio with denominator one hundred, no more.
- **Hypotenuse** — Greek *hypoteinousa*, "stretching under" — illuminates: the hypotenuse is the side stretching under the right angle, opposite to it.
- **Pythagorean** — from Pythagoras (6th c. BCE), though Babylonian texts predate the formal proof by ~1000 years.

## Trade-offs named

- **Strategy: discipline vs. overhead.** Five steps catch errors; for one-step problems they are overkill.
- **Templates: speed vs. shallow application.** Recognizing percent / mixture / motion shapes solves problems fast but invites misapplication when the actual problem doesn't fit.
- **Euclidean geometry: exactness vs. domain restriction.** $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ is exact in the plane, fails on curved surfaces. Terrestrial-scale problems are essentially planar; map-scale problems are not.

## Scale shifts used

- **Terrestrial Euclidean (rectangles, ladders) ↔ planetary spherical (continental surveying)** — purpose: shows that the formulas of this chapter are the planar special case of a larger story. Anchored in §4.

## Worked examples

- **Three consecutive integers summing to 96** — concept: naming variables to encode relationships — reusable as: any "consecutive" or "in-arithmetic-progression" problem.
- **Pharmacist combining 50% saline with water** — concept: mixture template — reusable as: ch 5 (solved via system), ch 8 (rational-form mixture problems).
- **Two cars going opposite directions** — concept: uniform motion with distances added — reusable as: ch 5 (system formulation), ch 10 (with quadratic constraint).
- **Rectangle with length-width relationship** — concept: geometry template + variable substitution — reusable as: ch 7 (factoring with area constraint), ch 10 (quadratic from area).
- **Round-trip driving with two speeds** — concept: motion problem that produces a quadratic — reusable as: a chapter 10 worked example.

## Reusable structural moves

- **"Recognize the template, write the equation"** — paired with the warning that templates can substitute for understanding. Reusable wherever a known shape simplifies setup.
- **Translation tables for inequality language** — *at least* vs. *more than*. Reusable for any constraint-language treatment.
- **The reflexive note on Euclidean geometry** — naming a system as one of many. Reusable wherever a "rule" turns out to be system-specific (matrix algebra, modular arithmetic, finite fields).
