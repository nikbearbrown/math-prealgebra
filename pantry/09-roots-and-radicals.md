## Analogies

- **Square root as inverse of squaring** — explains: why $\sqrt{a^2} = a$ for nonneg $a$ — limits: collapses for negative $a$, where $\sqrt{a^2} = |a|$.
- **Radical algebra = fractional-exponent algebra** — explains: why radical rules echo exponent rules from chapter 6 — limits: only fully visible after college-algebra-level fractional exponents are introduced.
- **Squaring as irreversible step (parallel to clearing denominators)** — explains: why extraneous solutions can arise — limits: works when paired with chapter 8; isolated in this chapter, the pattern is fresh.

## Etymologies

- **Radical** — Latin *radix* (root) — illuminates: a radical sign represents a root.
- **Radicand** — Latin *radicandum* (thing to be rooted) — illuminates: the radicand is the expression *to be rooted*.
- **Conjugate** — Latin *conjugare* (to yoke together) — illuminates: $a + b\sqrt{c}$ and $a - b\sqrt{c}$ are paired by sign-flip.
- **Principal** — Latin *principalis* (foremost) — illuminates: the principal square root is the *primary* of the two.

## Trade-offs named

- **Principal-root convention: simplicity vs. completeness.** Letting $\sqrt{}$ denote only the nonneg root simplifies notation but loses one of two solutions; equations with $\pm\sqrt{}$ recover both.
- **Squaring both sides: powerful but irreversible.** Removes the radical at the cost of possibly introducing extraneous solutions. Always check.
- **Rationalize-the-denominator convention: standardization vs. historical artifact.** Persists for comparability of answers; the original computational reason has lapsed.

## Scale shifts used

- **Single radical equation (one cliff stone) ↔ a class of inverse problems (pendulum, planetary orbit, statistical SD)** — purpose: shows that radical algebra is the language of *inverse* relationships in physics. Anchored in §5.
- **Reversible algebra (ch 1–7) ↔ irreversible algebra (ch 8 onward)** — purpose: continues the chapter 8 reflexive note; this is the second irreversibility setting. Anchored in §4.

## Worked examples

- **Simplifying $\sqrt{50} = 5\sqrt{2}$, $\sqrt{72} = 6\sqrt{2}$** — concept: extract perfect-square factors.
- **Simplifying $\sqrt{50x^3}$** — concept: variable radicands.
- **Adding $\sqrt{50} + \sqrt{8} = 7\sqrt{2}$** — concept: simplify first to find like radicals.
- **Multiplying $(2\sqrt{3})(5\sqrt{6}) = 30\sqrt{2}$** — concept: coefficient × coefficient, radical × radical, simplify.
- **Rationalizing $\dfrac{3}{\sqrt{2}} = \dfrac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}$** — concept: one-term denominator.
- **Rationalizing $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3} + 1} = \dfrac{\sqrt{3} - 1}{2}$** — concept: conjugate; difference of squares from ch 7.
- **Solving $\sqrt{x + 1} = 4 \to x = 15$** — concept: clean radical equation.
- **Solving $\sqrt{x} = -3 \to$ no solution** — concept: extraneous from squaring; principal-root convention.
- **Solving $\sqrt{2x + 5} - x = 1 \to x = 2$** (with $-2$ extraneous) — concept: check both roots.
- **Pendulum length for $T = 1$ s** — concept: radical equation in physical context.

## Reusable structural moves

- **"Always simplify first"** — discipline reusable in any add/subtract operation where like-terms detection requires preprocessing.
- **"Squaring is irreversible — check"** — second occurrence of the irreversibility/check pattern. Reusable wherever a power or transcendental operation may introduce extraneous solutions.
- **The principal-root note** — small notational choice that simplifies an entire body of mathematics. Reusable as a "convention earns its place" example.
