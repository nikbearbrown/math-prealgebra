## Analogies

- **Polynomials as the algebra of non-linear scaling** — explains: why we need polynomials at all — limits: not all non-linear scaling is polynomial (exponential, logarithmic, trigonometric).
- **FOIL as distributive property applied twice** — explains: why FOIL is not a separate rule — limits: FOIL works only for binomial × binomial; for other products, "distribute every term" is the more general phrasing.
- **Scientific notation as 'compressed' big and small numbers** — explains: why $5.972 \times 10^{24}$ is more manageable than 25 digits — limits: glosses the precision implications (significant figures).

## Etymologies

- **Polynomial / monomial / binomial / trinomial** — Greek *poly-* (many), *mono-* (one), *bi-* (two), *tri-* (three) + Latin *nōmen* (name) — illuminates: each is *N named pieces*.
- **Coefficient** — Latin *co-* (together) + *efficient* (effective) — the coefficient and the variable work together to produce the term.
- **Exponent** — Latin *exponere* (to put out, to display) — the exponent is the number "displayed" above the base.
- **Quadratic / cubic / quartic** — from squares (degree 2 = related to area), cubes (degree 3 = volume), and the Latin *quartus* (fourth).

## Trade-offs named

- **Polynomials: expressive power vs. arithmetic complexity.** They describe nearly all useful non-linear relationships, at the cost of more bookkeeping.
- **FOIL: speed vs. specificity.** Fastest way to multiply two binomials; doesn't generalize to longer products.
- **Scientific notation: convenience vs. abstraction.** Makes giant and tiny numbers tractable; slightly distances the user from the number's actual magnitude.

## Scale shifts used

- **Pizza area (10-inch vs. 14-inch) ↔ planetary masses ($5.972 \times 10^{24}$ kg) ↔ subatomic masses ($9.109 \times 10^{-31}$ kg)** — purpose: shows that polynomial-and-exponent algebra is the language of all scales of physical reality. Anchored across §1 and §4.
