## Scenes

- **Two paving machines working on a parking lot** — used in §1 cold open and §4 worked example — captures *rates of work add* concretely. Reusable in any work-rate or parallel-throughput context (computers, pipes, painting crews).
- **Boat going downstream/upstream with a 2 mph current** — used in §5 synthesis — different from the chapter 5 boat-with-current (which produced a system); here, equal-time formulation produces a single rational equation. Reusable for any "two equal times, different speeds" problem.

## Analogies

- **Rational expression as polynomial-over-polynomial fraction** — explains: why fraction-arithmetic rules carry over — limits: collapses on the *domain* issue (variables can be in denominators).
- **Cancellation only of factors, never terms** — explains: why $\frac{x+6}{6}$ does not simplify — limits: needs constant reinforcement; many students intuit the wrong rule.
- **Extraneous solutions as "irreversible step" residue** — explains: why checking is required — limits: stretches when the irreversible step is more abstract (e.g., taking a logarithm of a negative number in college algebra).

## Etymologies

- **Rational** — Latin *ratio* (reckoning, ratio) — illuminates: a rational expression is *of a ratio*.
- **Domain** — Latin *dominium* (lordship, ownership) — illuminates: the domain is the set of values the function "owns" or *is defined for*.
- **Extraneous** — Latin *extraneus* (foreign, strange) — illuminates: an extraneous solution is one that does not belong to the original problem.
- **LCD** — abbreviation; "least common denominator."

## Trade-offs named

- **Rational expressions: descriptive power vs. domain bookkeeping.** Lets you talk about rates and ratios; forces constant attention to where the expression is defined.
- **LCD method: reliability vs. procedural overhead.** Always works; computationally expensive on complex problems.
- **Reversible vs. irreversible solving steps.** Chapters 1–7 used only reversible moves; chapter 8 introduces irreversible steps, with the check as the cost.

## Scale shifts used

- **Two paving machines (concrete) ↔ general rate-addition (abstract)** — purpose: generalizes from one problem to a class. Anchored in §1 and §4.
- **Reversible algebra (ch 1–7) ↔ irreversible algebra (ch 8 onward)** — purpose: places the chapter as a transition point. Anchored in §4 reflexive note.

## Worked examples

- **Domain of $\frac{x+3}{x-2}$: $x \ne 2$** — concept: identifying forbidden values.
- **Simplifying $\frac{x^2-4}{x^2+5x+6}$ to $\frac{x-2}{x+3}$** — concept: factor and cancel; domain expansion.
- **Multiplying / dividing rational expressions** — concepts: factor before multiplying.
- **Adding $\frac{2}{x} + \frac{3}{x+1}$ via LCD** — concept: LCD method.
- **Solving $\frac{1}{x-2} = \frac{3}{x+4} \to x = 5$** — concept: clear denominators, check.
- **Extraneous-solution example with $x = 3$** — concept: candidate that satisfies cleared equation but not original.
- **Paving machines (rates add)** — concept: rational equation from rate-addition.
- **Boat with current (equal times)** — concept: rational equation from time-equality formulation.

## Reusable structural moves

- **"Factor first, then cancel"** — universal first instruction for rational expressions.
- **"Always check every candidate"** — discipline reusable for any irreversible step.
- **The reversible/irreversible reflexive note** — sets up chapters 9 and 10 as continuations of the same pattern.
- **"Different formulations, same answer"** (§5 synthesis) — useful for any problem that admits multiple algebraic setups (cf. boat-with-current in chapters 3, 5, and 8).
