## Analogies

- **Slope as rate of trade-off** — explains: why a constant rate produces a straight line — limits: breaks down for nonlinear relationships where the rate varies (calculus).
- **Three forms, one line ("same line, three dresses")** — explains: why slope-intercept, point-slope, and standard form describe the same object — limits: stretches when introducing more exotic forms (parametric, polar).
- **Parallel lines as same-slope/different-intercept; perpendicular as negative-reciprocal** — explains: the geometric content of those algebraic conditions — limits: negative-reciprocal formula breaks at horizontal/vertical pair (boundary case).

## Etymologies

- **Cartesian** — from René Descartes (1637, *La Géométrie*), who formalized coordinate geometry.
- **Abscissa / ordinate** — Latin *abscissa linea* ("cut-off line") and *ordinata* ("ordered") — historical names for the x- and y-coordinates.
- **Slope** — from Old English *slūpan* (to glide, to slip); the letter *m* may come from French *monter* (to climb), though attribution is unsettled.
- **Quadrant** — Latin *quadrans* (a fourth) — one of the four quarters of the plane.

## Trade-offs named

- **Graph: speed of seeing vs. precision of numbers.** Picture is fast, equation is exact. You need both.
- **Slope-intercept form: readability vs. universality.** Best form for non-vertical lines; doesn't apply to vertical lines.
- **Linear models: simplicity vs. extrapolation danger.** Two numbers describe a line; the line is unreliable far from the data that built it.
- **Euclidean parallel/perpendicular: local exactness vs. global generality.** Works on the flat plane; fails on spheres and saddles.

## Scale shifts used

- **Local linear models (heating bill) ↔ extrapolation collapse far from data** — purpose: shows why linearity is a local approximation and why global use is dangerous. Anchored in §5.
- **2,000-year reign of Euclidean geometry ↔ 19th-century non-Euclidean revolution** — purpose: places the chapter's content in a larger mathematical history. Anchored in §4.

## Worked examples

- **Graphing $y = 2x - 1$ by plotting points** — concept: linear equation in two variables → table of solutions → straight line.
- **Slope from two points: $(2,3)$ and $(5,9)$** — concept: $m = \Delta y / \Delta x$.
- **Line through $(2, 7)$ with slope $4$ in three forms** — concept: form interconversion.
- **Line perpendicular to $y = -\frac{1}{2}x + 7$ through $(3, 4)$** — concept: negative reciprocal slope + point-slope form.
- **Heating-bill linear model** — concept: real-world linear fit, slope and intercept interpretation, extrapolation warning.

## Reusable structural moves

- **"Three forms, one line"** — taxonomic move; reusable wherever multiple representations describe the same object.
- **"Linear models are local approximations"** — caveat reusable in any data-fitting context, including statistics and simple regression.
- **The reflexive note on Euclidean geometry** (parallels OpenStax's silence on this point) — naming the geometry as one of many. Reusable in chapters where a "rule" is system-specific.
