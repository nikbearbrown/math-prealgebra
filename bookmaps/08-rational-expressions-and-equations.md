## Deferred material

- **Complex rational expressions** (m82537) — fractions whose numerators or denominators *are themselves* rational expressions. Useful but specialized; deferred to college algebra or as an instructor-supplied appendix. Reason: the techniques (write as division, or multiply by LCD) duplicate methods already in the chapter; including them would dilute the core focus.
- **Proportions and similar-figures applications** (m82539) — proportions appear briefly in §4, but the geometric similar-figures content (scale drawings, indirect measurement) is deferred. Reason: scale-up and similarity belong with geometry and trigonometry, not the rational-expressions spine.
- **Variation: direct, inverse, joint** (m82560) — Boyle's Law, Hooke's Law, intensity-distance relationships. Deferred. Reason: variation is a topic of its own and benefits from a more developed function-notation framework, which is treated in college algebra. The chapter focuses on equation-solving rather than function modeling.
- **Multiple work-rate worked examples** (m82543) — compressed to two: paving machines and the boat-with-current. Reason: pattern is the same across all such problems; one or two clean examples suffice to teach the setup.
- **Detailed treatment of opposite factors** (m82532, simplification by recognizing $a - b = -(b - a)$) — referenced briefly but not given a worked example. Reason: useful trick, but not foundational.

## Source-level notes

- **No contradictions** between source files.
- **Extraneous-solution definition.** Source treats extraneous solutions concretely (with examples) but does not name the underlying issue (irreversibility of multiplication by a possibly-zero expression). The rewrite adds this framing in §4.
- **Paving machines synthesis (§1, §4).** Original construction. Times $6$ and $4$ chosen to give a clean answer ($t = 12/5 = 2.4$ hours) and to illustrate the LCD = 12 work cleanly.
- **Boat synthesis (§5).** Original construction; standard equal-time-current setup. Speed $b = 10$ mph and current $2$ mph chosen for clean integer-arithmetic checks.
- **Connection to chapter 5 boat problem.** Cross-referenced explicitly to highlight that the same physical situation can be modeled by either a system of two linear equations (chapter 5) or a single rational equation (chapter 8).
