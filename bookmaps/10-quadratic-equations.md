## Deferred material

- **Complex solutions** — touched only in Challenge exercise 14 with a forward pointer to college algebra. Reason: complex numbers are a topic of their own; introducing them in passing would either be misleading or take too long.
- **Vertex form $y = a(x - h)^2 + k$** — referenced implicitly via completing the square but not given separate treatment. Reason: vertex form is the standard form of a parabola in college algebra; the rewrite stops at standard form $y = ax^2 + bx + c$ with vertex computed via $-b/(2a)$.
- **Specific business and physics applications** (m82558) — many worked examples in source. Compressed in the rewrite to one (the toy rocket in §4) and one synthesis (widget pricing in §5). The remaining applications belong in exercises.
- **Geometric area-model derivation** of completing the square — alluded to but not depicted. A designer could reinstate.

## Source-level notes

- **No contradictions** between source files.
- **Pedagogical sequencing.** Source orders: Square Root Property → completing the square → quadratic formula → applications → graphing. Rewrite preserves this order under three concepts: (1) [SR property + complete the square], (2) [formula + discriminant], (3) [graphing]. Applications are threaded throughout rather than isolated.
- **Quadratic formula derivation.** Original to the rewrite as a *sketch*; the OpenStax m82557 includes a more detailed step-by-step derivation. The rewrite compresses to the key algebraic moves.
- **Basketball cold open (§1).** Original; coefficients $-0.04, 0.5, 6$ are physical estimates, not lifted from a specific source. The pedagogical point — *the math says no, the ball is below the rim* — uses the equation as a real predictive instrument.
- **Widget pricing synthesis (§5).** Original construction. Coefficients $-5, 240, -1600$ chosen to factor cleanly, give clean integer break-even points (8 and 40), and have an integer vertex (24, 1280) for clean exposition.
- **Abel-Ruffini reference** — well-attested mathematical-history claim (proved by Niels Henrik Abel in 1824, building on Paolo Ruffini's earlier incomplete proof). The rewrite uses it as a single-paragraph reflexive note.
