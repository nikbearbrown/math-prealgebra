## Deferred material

- **Linear inequalities in two variables (m82488)** — the entire 5,250-word treatment of inequality regions, boundary lines, and shading is deferred to chapter 5, where it integrates naturally with systems of inequalities. A brief Challenge exercise (#13) previews the technique but does not develop it. Reason: chapter 4's spine is *the line and its equation*; introducing inequality regions here would make the chapter's central object ambiguous (line vs. half-plane).
- **Geoboards as physical models** (m82483) — pedagogically useful in classrooms with the equipment, but the rewrite uses verbal rise/run framing instead.
- **Worked applications of slope-intercept form to specific real-world contexts** (m82485 has many) — the rewrite uses the heating-bill example in §5 as the canonical application; OpenStax's parallel examples (cell phone bills, gym memberships, tipping schedules) are deferred to a future revision or to instructor-supplied examples.
- **Detailed treatment of the x-intercept method as a standalone technique** (m82482) — compressed in the rewrite to a single mention in §3 and a single exercise. Reason: intercept method is computationally identical to plotting two specific points; once slope-intercept form is mastered, the standalone method is redundant.

## Source-level notes

- **No contradictions** between source files.
- **Slope formula** ($m = (y_2 - y_1)/(x_2 - x_1)$) is universal and unambiguous in source.
- **Letter *m* for slope** — historical attribution debated. The "from French *monter*" hypothesis is widely cited but unverified; OpenStax does not weigh in. Rewrite mentions it as "possibly" with a hedge.
- **Cartesian historical claim.** Descartes published *La Géométrie* in 1637, which is well-attested. The naval-officer-and-lighthouse opener is a constructed scene; no specific historical incident is claimed.
- **Heating-bill synthesis problem.** Original construction. Numbers chosen to be exactly collinear so that the rewrite can illustrate the linear-model fit cleanly; a real dataset would not be perfectly collinear, and the rewrite acknowledges this in §5.
