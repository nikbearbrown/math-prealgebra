## Figure 1: Strict vs. non-strict inequality on a number line

**Placement:** §4 (Concept 3 — Inequalities), at the end of the "Common misconceptions" subsection.

**Description:** Two horizontal number lines stacked vertically, both ranging from approximately −2 to +6 with integer ticks.

**Top line:** An open circle (○) at 4. Shading extends from the open circle leftward to the left edge of the line, ending in an arrow indicating "continues to negative infinity." Below the line, two labels: "$x < 4$" centered, and "$(-\infty, 4)$" beneath that.

**Bottom line:** A closed (filled) circle (●) at 3. Shading extends from the closed circle leftward to the left edge of the line, ending in an arrow. Below the line: "$x \le 3$" and "$(-\infty, 3]$".

A short caption underneath both: *Open circle and parenthesis exclude the boundary; closed circle and bracket include it.*

**Pedagogical purpose:** Establish in one image the visual distinction between strict ($<$, $>$) and non-strict ($\le$, $\ge$) inequalities. The contrast is the key teaching moment — students often conflate the two notations or invert them.

**Style notes:** Black ink on white background. Number lines at the same scale. Open circles drawn as a hollow circle with a clear interior; filled circles as solid black dots. Arrow tips on the left ends, indicating extension to negative infinity. Sans-serif labels in mathematical notation. Minimum tick spacing such that 4 and 3 are visibly distinct on each line.

**Source link:** Conceptually parallels OpenStax figures from m82470 illustrating inequality graphs, but combined into a single side-by-side comparison.
