## Figure 1: The three outcomes of a linear system

**Placement:** §2 (Concept 1), at the end of the "three geometric outcomes" subsection.

**Description:** Three small coordinate planes arranged side-by-side horizontally, each with x and y axes from approximately −5 to +5.

**Plane A (left):** Two distinct lines intersecting at one visible point (e.g., $y = x + 1$ and $y = -x + 5$ crossing at $(2, 3)$). Mark the intersection with a solid dot. Caption underneath: *"(A) one solution — consistent, independent."*

**Plane B (center):** Two parallel lines with the same slope but different y-intercepts (e.g., $y = 2x + 1$ and $y = 2x - 2$). No intersection. Caption: *"(B) no solution — inconsistent."*

**Plane C (right):** Two coincident lines drawn as a single thick line, with both equations labeled (e.g., $y = 2x + 1$ and $4x - 2y = -2$). Caption: *"(C) infinitely many solutions — consistent, dependent."*

**Pedagogical purpose:** Place the three outcomes side-by-side so the geometric distinction is unmistakable. Students who see the parallel-lines case and the coincident-lines case adjacent stop confusing inconsistent with dependent.

**Style notes:** Three small panels, each ~2.5" square. Equal axis scales across all three. Lines drawn at slightly different angles in panel A and slightly more separated y-intercepts in panel B for visual clarity. Panel C should be unmistakably *one* line, not two slightly offset; the dual labeling is what conveys the dependent relationship.

**Source link:** Conceptually parallels OpenStax m82490 figures showing graph-method examples of each outcome, consolidated.
