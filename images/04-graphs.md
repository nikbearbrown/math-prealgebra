## Figure 1: Cartesian coordinate plane with four quadrants

**Placement:** §2 (Concept 1 — The coordinate plane), after the conventions paragraph.

**Description:** A Cartesian coordinate plane spanning approximately $-5$ to $+5$ on both axes. Both axes labeled at the right end (x-axis) and top (y-axis), with arrows. Tick marks at every integer. The origin is labeled $(0, 0)$ at the intersection.

The four quadrants are labeled with Roman numerals in their respective regions:
- **I** (upper right, both coordinates positive)
- **II** (upper left, x negative, y positive)
- **III** (lower left, both coordinates negative)
- **IV** (lower right, x positive, y negative)

Four points plotted, one in each quadrant, each labeled with its ordered pair:
- $(3, 5)$ in I
- $(-2, 4)$ in II
- $(-3, -1)$ in III
- $(4, -2)$ in IV

**Pedagogical purpose:** Establish, in one image, all the conventions of the coordinate plane: the two perpendicular axes, the origin, the counterclockwise quadrant numbering, and the (x, y) ordered-pair convention. This is the single most-referenced figure in the chapter.

**Style notes:** Clean line art. Axes drawn with arrows pointing to positive directions. Integer tick marks small but visible. Quadrant labels in a slightly faded font, positioned centrally within each quadrant rather than at the perimeter. Plotted points as solid black dots with their coordinate labels positioned to the upper-right of each dot to avoid overlap. Equal scale on both axes.

**Source link:** Conceptually parallels OpenStax m82480 figure CNX_ElemAlg_Figure_04_01_001 (and follow-ups). The rewrite consolidates several source figures into this single canonical reference.
